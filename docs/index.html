The three costs of owning an ETF

Two of these are invisible on any price chart. A cheap headline fee means little if the fund's domicile quietly taxes your dividends and your estate.
1 · Expense ratio (TER)
The fund's annual management fee, taken from returns daily. Broad index ETFs run 0.05–0.30%; niche/thematic funds 0.45–0.80%.
2 · Dividend withholding
Tax withheld on dividends inside the fund. A US-domiciled fund suffers 30% on US dividends for a Singaporean; an Irish UCITS suffers 15% (treaty). Permanent drag.
3 · US estate tax
On death, US-situs assets above US$60k are taxed up to 40% before heirs inherit. Follows the asset, not your broker. Avoided entirely with non-US-domiciled funds.

How to implement

    The 40% trap: US estate tax for Singapore investors

    Singapore abolished estate duty in 2008, so most of us assume inheritance is tax-free. On the Singapore side, it is. The trap is on the US side — and it lands on the assets many of us hold most: US stocks and US-domiciled ETFs. A plain-language summary of the issue and the fixes. Educational only — not tax or legal advice.

    The exemption gap

    A US citizen can die with US$15 million in assets before US estate tax bites. A Singaporean gets US$60,000 — an exemption 250× smaller. Above it, the tax is graduated from 18% up to 40%.

    US citizen exemption
    US$15m
    Singaporean exemption
    US$60k

    How the tax scales (no treaty)

    On a US$1m US-stock portfolio the estate tax is about US$332,800 — an effective 33%. Singapore has no US estate-tax treaty, so there is no relief beyond the US$60k.

    What is exposed, and what is safe

    US-situs — exposed

    • US-listed stocks (Apple, Nvidia, Tesla…)
    • US-domiciled ETFs (VOO, SPY, QQQ — and SGX's S27, D07, GSD)
    • Cash sitting in a US brokerage account
    • US retirement accounts (IRA / 401k)
    • US real estate

    Not US-situs — safe

    • Irish / other non-US-domiciled ETFs (e.g. CSPX)
    • Non-US and SGX-listed shares
    • Cash in a US bank deposit account
    • Most directly-held US Treasuries
    • Non-US real estate
    Your broker does not protect you. US estate tax follows the underlying asset — situs is set by where the company is incorporated, not where your brokerage (IBKR, Tiger, moomoo, Saxo) or even your SRS account sits. And it is enforced: on death the US custodian freezes the account and requires an IRS transfer certificate (Form 5173) before releasing anything — a process that routinely takes 6–18 months.

    The fixes, ranked by how practical they are

    1
    Use Irish-domiciled UCITS ETFs the clean fix
    For index exposure this solves it outright. VOO → CSPX (iShares Core S&P 500 UCITS, Ireland, London-listed): zero US estate-tax exposure at any size, and 15% dividend withholding instead of 30% (US–Ireland treaty). Slightly higher fee, slightly less liquidity — worth it for most. This is the whole basis of the tool's core-satellite split.
    2
    Gift the shares while you are alive
    A Singaporean's gift of US shares falls outside US gift tax entirely (intangible property). The catch: it is irrevocable, and the exposure transfers to your children rather than disappearing. A genuine tool for older investors sitting on large, appreciated positions.
    3
    Stay under US$60,000
    Below the exemption there is no tax and no filing. Fine when you are starting out — but treat it as a phase, not a plan.
    4
    Life insurance — to fund the bill, not dodge it
    Death-benefit payouts are generally outside the US estate. This does not remove the liability; it gives your estate the cash to pay it without a forced sale of your holdings.
    5
    Foreign company or trust >US$5m · get advice
    Holding US assets through a foreign company or trust can move them out of your US estate. Costly and complex — not a DIY job. If you are at that size, see a cross-border tax and legal adviser.
    Does not work: joint accounts (for non-spouse holders the IRS presumes the whole account belongs to the first to die) and the spousal exemption (it only applies when the surviving spouse is a US citizen).
