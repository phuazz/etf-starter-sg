Ready to act? Your first purchase, step by step
  1. Pick one broker that can reach both markets. The starter mixes use funds listed in London (the Irish UCITS core) and on SGX. Interactive Brokers, Tiger, moomoo and Saxo all reach both; any of them works. SGX purchases sit in CDP or the broker’s custody depending on the broker; London funds are always held in custody. Compare commissions and currency-conversion costs, not sign-up gifts.
  2. Fund the account in SGD. A FAST transfer from your bank is free and arrives in minutes at most brokers.
  3. Convert currency before buying the London funds — this is where beginners silently overpay. The core UCITS funds trade in US dollars. Currency conversion costs anywhere from a few hundredths of a percent at the cheapest brokers (roughly S$2–5 on S$10,000) to 0.5% or more through bank-card or wire routes (S$50+ on the same amount) — on the cheap core funds, a bad conversion can cost more than a whole year of fund fees. Convert inside the brokerage at its stated rate, and check the fee schedule first. Typical ranges as of mid-2026 — verify your broker’s current pricing.
  4. Choose the accumulating share class where offered. CSPX, VWRA, IWDA, EIMI and AGGU reinvest dividends inside the fund automatically — no idle cash, no further conversion cost, nothing for you to do. (Funds marked “Reinvested” in the table are these.)
  5. Place a limit order during market hours. Set your price at or just above the quoted asking price rather than using a market order — it costs nothing extra and protects you from a bad fill. London trades 8:00am–4:30pm UK time, which is roughly 3pm–11:30pm in Singapore during UK summer and 4pm–12:30am in UK winter. SGX trades 9am–5pm Singapore time. Note the per-share price: one share of CSPX runs to several hundred US dollars, so on small amounts use a broker with fractional shares, or a lower-priced fund such as VWRA.
  6. What a S$10,000 first purchase roughly costs, all-in. Commission of a few dollars, currency conversion from S$2 (good route) to S$50+ (bad route), and a bid-ask spread of a few hundredths of a percent on the liquid core funds — call it 0.1%–0.6% one-off depending mostly on your conversion route. The recurring costs this tool focuses on (fund fees and dividend withholding) matter more over the years, but a poor conversion route repeats on every top-up.
  7. Then set the routine and stop watching. Add a fixed amount monthly, rebalance roughly once a year back to your target weights, and treat market falls as expected weather rather than a reason to sell. The plan works through repetition, not timing.

After you buy: the four habits that do the real work

Picking the funds is the easy part. Nearly all of a long-term investor’s result comes from behaviour over the years that follow — and the winning behaviour is famously boring.

  1. Add money on a schedule, not on a hunch. Set a fixed amount every month and buy regardless of the headlines. This spreads your entry price over time and removes the impossible job of timing the market. Automate it if your broker allows.
  2. Rebalance about once a year. Winners grow to dominate the mix and quietly raise your risk. Once a year, sell a little of what has run and top up what has lagged to return to your target weights — or simply steer new contributions toward the underweight funds, which avoids selling and its costs. A calendar reminder is enough; there is no need to watch markets.
  3. Expect the falls — they are the price of the returns, not a malfunction. A globally diversified portfolio can drop 30% or more in a bad year; it has happened repeatedly and will again. The investors who do well are the ones who keep buying through it. Selling in a fall converts a paper dip into a permanent loss. If a plausible fall would make you sell, you are holding too much equity — dial back to a calmer mix in the Build tab now, while calm.
  4. Leave it alone, and ignore the noise. Checking daily and reacting to news is where returns go to die. Review once or twice a year against your plan — not against the latest hot fund. Costs, taxes and time in the market are the levers you control; next quarter’s price is not.

The three costs of owning an ETF

Two of these are invisible on any price chart. A cheap headline fee means little if the fund's domicile quietly taxes your dividends and your estate.
1 · Expense ratio (TER)
The fund's annual management fee, taken from returns daily. Broad index ETFs run 0.05–0.30%; niche/thematic funds 0.45–0.80%.
2 · Dividend withholding
Tax withheld on dividends inside the fund. A US-domiciled fund suffers 30% on US dividends for a Singaporean; an Irish UCITS suffers 15% (treaty). Permanent drag.
3 · US estate tax
On death, US-situs assets above US$60k are taxed up to 40% before heirs inherit. Follows the asset, not your broker. Avoided entirely with non-US-domiciled funds.

How to implement

    The 40% trap: US estate tax for Singapore investors

    Singapore abolished estate duty in 2008, so most of us assume inheritance is tax-free. On the Singapore side, it is. The trap is on the US side — and it lands on the assets many of us hold most: US stocks and US-domiciled ETFs. A plain-language summary of the issue and the fixes. Educational only — not tax or legal advice.

    The exemption gap

    A US citizen can die with US$15 million in assets before US estate tax bites. A Singaporean gets US$60,000 — an exemption 250× smaller. Above it, the tax is graduated from 18% up to 40%.

    US citizen exemption
    US$15m
    Singaporean exemption
    US$60k

    How the tax scales (no treaty)

    On a US$1m US-stock portfolio the estate tax is about US$332,800 — an effective 33%. Singapore has no US estate-tax treaty, so there is no relief beyond the US$60k.

    Estimate the tax on your US-situs holdings

    US-situs = US-domiciled ETFs (SGX's S27, D07, GSD) plus any directly-held US shares. S$ figures assume S$1 ≈ US$0.74 — an assumption; the live rate moves.

    What is exposed, and what is safe

    US-situs — exposed

    • US-listed stocks (Apple, Nvidia, Tesla…)
    • US-domiciled ETFs (VOO, SPY, QQQ — and SGX's S27, D07, GSD)
    • Cash sitting in a US brokerage account
    • US retirement accounts (IRA / 401k)
    • US real estate

    Not US-situs — safe

    • Irish / other non-US-domiciled ETFs (e.g. CSPX)
    • Non-US and SGX-listed shares
    • Cash in a US bank deposit account
    • Most directly-held US Treasuries
    • Non-US real estate
    Your broker does not protect you. US estate tax follows the underlying asset — situs is set by where the company is incorporated, not where your brokerage (IBKR, Tiger, moomoo, Saxo) or even your SRS account sits. And it is enforced: on death the US custodian freezes the account and requires an IRS transfer certificate (Form 5173) before releasing anything — a process that routinely takes 6–18 months.

    The fixes, ranked by how practical they are

    1
    Use Irish-domiciled UCITS ETFs the clean fix
    For index exposure this solves it outright. VOO → CSPX (iShares Core S&P 500 UCITS, Ireland, London-listed): zero US estate-tax exposure at any size, and 15% dividend withholding instead of 30% (US–Ireland treaty). Slightly higher fee, slightly less liquidity — worth it for most. This is the whole basis of the tool's core-satellite split.
    2
    Gift the shares while you are alive
    A Singaporean's gift of US shares falls outside US gift tax entirely (intangible property). The catch: it is irrevocable, and the exposure transfers to your children rather than disappearing. A genuine tool for older investors sitting on large, appreciated positions.
    3
    Stay under US$60,000
    Below the exemption there is no tax and no filing. Fine when you are starting out — but treat it as a phase, not a plan.
    4
    Life insurance — to fund the bill, not dodge it
    Death-benefit payouts are generally outside the US estate. This does not remove the liability; it gives your estate the cash to pay it without a forced sale of your holdings.
    5
    Foreign company or trust >US$5m · get advice
    Holding US assets through a foreign company or trust can move them out of your US estate. Costly and complex — not a DIY job. If you are at that size, see a cross-border tax and legal adviser.
    Does not work: joint accounts (for non-spouse holders the IRS presumes the whole account belongs to the first to die) and the spousal exemption (it only applies when the surviving spouse is a US citizen).

    Cash, SRS or CPF-OA — which pocket should you invest from?

    Singapore gives you three pockets that can buy investments, and they behave very differently on tax, lock-up and what they are allowed to hold. Most beginners should understand all three before moving money.

    A common-sense order many beginners follow
    1. Emergency fund first — three to six months of expenses in cash or a money-market fund (e.g. MMS), before any investing.
    2. Invest cash through the starter plans — full flexibility, no lock-up, and access to the estate-tax-clean Irish UCITS core.
    3. Add SRS if you pay meaningful income tax and can genuinely leave the money until 64ish — the relief is the whole point, and the account must then be invested (SGX funds such as ES3 or MBH), not left at 0.05%.
    4. Touch CPF-OA last, if at all — above the S$20,000 buffer, only where you expect a diversified holding to beat the guaranteed 2.5–3.5% after fees. Leaving OA untouched is itself a respectable bond-like allocation.
    A common ordering, not personalised advice — your tax bracket, horizon and employer situation change the answer.
